Creating a Japanese Style Garden on a Budget in Calgary in 2024

By Jose G. Monagas, December 2023

As the average house price in Calgary is expected to be $564,000 at the end of 2023, a Japanese style landscape can significantly boost your property's value.

You may need to hire a landscape architect to help you design your Japanese garden, choose the right plants, layout, and structures. The cost for this service can range from $2,000 to $6,000.

Next, you will need a garden design plan to guide the landscaping crews during the construction phase. The cost for this can range from $2,000 to $6,000.

The choice of plants and trees is a crucial aspect of a Japanese style garden. Depending on the species and quantity you choose, this could cost you between $5,000 and $30,000. We recommend you consult with a local nursery or horticultural expert when planning, as Calgary's climate may not be suitable for all species.

Your shortlist of plants could include: 10 species of lilacs (for their fragrant flowers that bloom in the spring); 10 species of maples (for their vibrant fall colours); 10 species of bushes (for their hardiness and suitability for gardens in Calgary); 10 species of pines (for their year round greenery); and 10 species of caraganas (for their ability to survive in harsh climates).

Rocks and gravel are essential elements of Japanese gardens. These could cost you from $2,000 to $6,000. Adding fixed structures such as stone lanterns, entrance gates, and fences can add to the authenticity of your Japanese style garden. These structures could cost between $5,000 and $20,000. The cost of transporting and delivering materials to your home can range from $2,000 to $6,000.

The labour costs for landscaping crews can vary greatly, depending on the complexity of the design and the size of your garden. These costs could range from $10,000 to $50,000. Maintaining a Japanese garden might be challenging for you alone. Thus, you may need to budget between $1,000 and $6,000 for yearly maintenance and upkeep.

There Are Several Other Factors to Consider When Budgeting for Your Japanese Garden

Depending on the scale of your project and local regulations, you may need to obtain City permits. The cost of these permits can vary. If your garden design includes water features or outdoor lighting, there will be utility costs to consider, unless you use solar powered features.

As some plants may not survive the winter, you may need to budget for replacing plants or providing them with winter protection. Pests can be a problem in any garden, and you may need to budget for pest control measures.

If you are not a gardening expert, you might need to hire a professional for regular maintenance tasks like pruning, shaping, weeding, etc. Depending on the value of your garden, especially with expensive structures or rare plants, you might want to consider getting them insured. What Is The Secret Behind Japanese Zen Dry Gardens?

By Jose G. Monagas, December 2023

It may be just a trend, but residents in Calgary are talking about Japanese Zen dry gardens, and wonder what makes a Zen garden so different from western style gardens with lawns and flowers.

For some, Zen dry gardens look simple and rather monochromatic. But, undoubtedly, this apparent simplicity can be quite deceptive; thus comparable to an iceberg, where much of the substance lies beneath the surface.

In my personal experience of over a decade of continuous search and learning, I have found that Zen dry gardens are no less than complex spaces with an all encompassing meaning that is as thought provoking and timeless as the pyramids for the ancient pharaohs and Egyptians. You will find in your own exploration journey that the concept of monotone (or limited color palette) in the context of Zen contributes to enhancing a garden's tranquility and aesthetic appeal.

At this point, some might argue that the pyramids and Zen dry gardens have absolutely nothing in common because of their geographical and cultural distance. But the truth is that both share the purpose of guiding a person's mind towards higher realms of thought. And here is why: The minimalist design of Zen dry gardens causes the observer to go deep within themselves, while reflecting upon the essence of nature and the universe. The gravel, rocks, boulders, stones, shrubs and moss are just the canvas for exploring these philosophical concepts.

In counter balance, the precise geometric design of the pyramids, with their celestial alignments and intricate internal structures, reflects the concept of balance and order as embodied by the ancient Egyptian goddess Ma'at. Therefore, the narrow ascending passage to the pharaoh's grand chamber in the pyramid can be regarded as the pathway leading to the ultimate liberation from the constraints of this physical world to the eternal or the afterlife.

In other words, both Zen dry gardens and pyramids can be viewed as mind tracks: Where Zen dry gardens lead the observer's mind on a path for contemplating the true nature of reality (i.e.: solitude and impermanence); while the pyramids help induce a person's focus on a journey about the immortality of their souls versus the apparent and transitory state of the deceased.

To make it short, the cultural and geographical differences of these two vehicles, with their exquisite and symbolic meanings, inspire introspection on the eternal journey of the soul. And that is their secret and what makes them so fascinating to me personally; not just the ancient civilizations that imbued them with their profound philosophies and religious beliefs.

So, when creating your Zen dry garden, take your time to explore different designs and choose the elements that resonate with you the most. Focus on creating a special space that will promote your peace and tranquility. Each garden presents its own unique challenges and rewards. Luckily, there are no strict rules to follow.

For example, the pure act of raking the sand (or pea gravel), and making patterns that reflect your mood and thoughts, can take your mind away of mundane problems, and help you relieve your stress. Yet, if you really do not enjoy this repetitive motion and focus, then you can simply sit quietly and breathe until your mind let go your anxiety.

The Joy of Self-Taught Japanese Zen Style Landscaping

By Jose G. Monagas, January 2024

For some of us, traditional college schooling or an apprenticeship can be quite formal and expensive. In contrast, self-learning and lifelong learning can be cost-effective, with so many open and affordable resources available, including books, manuals, online tutorials, forums, blogs, and more. The best learning method depends on your personal learning style, availability, and career goals.

Without the constraints of conventional schooling or an apprenticeship, you have the freedom to explore and experiment. You can learn at your own pace, focusing on those areas that interest you the most. This freedom fosters creativity and innovation, allowing you to develop your unique talents.

This makes Japanese style landscaping accessible to everyone, regardless of race, gender, income, and other circumstances. Without the pressure of grades and attendance, you can tailor your learning schedule according to your lifestyle. Whether you are a working professional, a retiree, or a stay-at-home parent, you can always find time to indulge in your passion for Japanese style landscaping. This also creates an educational environment that celebrates diversity and caters to the needs of every single learner.

More importantly, Japanese Zen style landscaping is more than just gardening — it is a blend of art and self-awareness. You learn to be patient, observant, and mindful. These requirements not only improve your landscaping skills but also contribute to your personal development. Every expert was once a novice. Thus, learning Japanese style landscaping as a self-taught individual can be a very rewarding experience.

In my case, I highly regard personalized learning and project-based learning. Through them, I can immerse myself on complex, real-world projects that require critical thinking, problem-solving, and collaboration with others, which allows me to explore topics of interest in greater depth. Naturally, there are times when I need a mentor who can provide me with guidance, inspiration, and practical knowledge.

Also, through hands-on learning, I can apply both my personal and professional experience as an invaluable tool in a field that is continually evolving, where practical skills are as important as theoretical knowledge.

As a non-Japanese in Calgary, I know firsthand that understanding the underlying principles of Japanese style landscaping requires time and energy — and some might find it quite challenging. However, anyone can do well as long as you have a deep respect for the culture, philosophy, and aesthetics while creating spaces that reflect the natural world and bring a sense of peace.

What is more, in navigating the challenges of self-learning traditional Japanese style landscaping in a foreign country, I have found that although there are numerous books and online resources on Japanese style landscaping, not all of them are comprehensive. In fact, some resources may oversimplify or misinterpret the principles of Japanese style landscaping. Another factor to consider is that there are not many experts in Japanese style landscaping outside of Japan; thus, some learners may struggle to grasp the intricate details of this art form.

Lastly, I cannot skip highlighting the differences in climate and plant availability between Japan and North America. You must be open to learn and adapt Japanese style landscaping principles to local conditions in Calgary. This involves choosing plant species that are similar to those used in Japanese gardens but are suited to the North American climate. You could also modify traditional techniques to work with the local environment where certain plant species may not thrive.

In my experience, self-learning Japanese Zen style landscaping in North America is a feasible and rewarding journey. The key is the ability to learn independently and apply this knowledge creatively. The belief that professional Japanese landscaping requires formal credentials stems from the respect for tradition and mastery. However, this perspective overlooks the potential of self-learning and the value of hands-on experience.
