Homeowners in Calgary are Looking for More Versatile and Low Maintenance Solutions to Traditional Lawns in their Yards

By Jose G. Monagas | November 2023 | Calgary, AB Canada

Introducing Japanese gardens and landscaping to residents in Calgary is an exciting venture. In prior years, both the news and the media have consistently reported that a typical household with a lawn consumes in excess of ten thousand gallons of water per year (a phenomenon known as "the hidden water waste in lawn maintenance").

More interestingly is the fact that in dry climates like Calgary, especially during the hot summer months, many homeowners water their lawns more than necessary in an effort to maintaining their "green" appearance. This undoubtedly leads to a great deal of water waste via evaporation.

Therefore, it is only rational to note that the adoption of dry gardens is nothing but one smart step towards a more sustainable future. Given that a dry garden is a landscape that is specifically created with drought tolerant plants that can survive with little to no irrigation, they are the perfect solution to "the water footprint of lawns in North America".

One of the top selling points of this type of landscaping style is the fact that a dry garden requires less care and attention than a traditional lawn; which is just perfect for those homeowners who have limited time and a budget for their upkeeping. This can transform any space into a peaceful retreat within their own homes.

Fortunately, here in Alberta, we have plants that can thrive in our climate and soil conditions while acting as eco-friendly ground cover. For example, our favorite replacement for lawn includes the "sedum" species (known as "stonecrops") and "woolly thyme" (known as "wild thyme").

Also, by incorporating key features like, for instance, pea gravel, rocks, stones and boulders, homeowners can add great value to the aesthetic appeal of their gardens while reducing the excessive need for water.

Japanese and Zen dry gardens are not only aesthetically appealing but also versatile. They can be designed to fit both outdoor and indoor spaces. With their adaptability, and low maintenance costs, they can contribute to increasing a real estate property resale value by 15% to 20%, which makes them a solid alternative for those homeowners who are considering long-term investment opportunities.

We consciously follow the principles of the craft while making the necessary adjustments to suit the climate and ecology of our region. Our work is a labor of love, with a deep appreciation of the Japanese aesthetics and the cultural values of simplicity and harmony with nature.

Creating a Japanese Style Garden on a Budget in Calgary in 2024

By Jose G. Monagas | December 2023 | Calgary, AB Canada

As the average house price in Calgary is expected to be $564,000 at the end of 2023, a Japanese style landscape can significantly boost your property's value.

You may need to hire a landscape architect to help you design your Japanese garden, choose the right plants, layout, and structures. The cost for this service can range from $2,000 to $6,000.

Next, you will need a garden design plan to guide the landscaping crews during the construction phase. The cost for this can range from $2,000 to $6,000.

The choice of plants and trees is a crucial aspect of a Japanese garden. Depending on the species and quantity you choose, this could cost you between $5,000 and $30,000.

We recommend you consult with a local nursery or horticultural expert when planning, as Calgary's climate may not be suitable for all species.

Your shortlist of plants could include: 10 species of lilacs (for their fragrant flowers that bloom in the spring); 10 species of maples (for their vibrant fall colours); 10 species of bushes (for their hardiness and suitability for gardens in Calgary); 10 species of pines (for their year round greenery); and 10 species of caraganas (for their ability to survive in harsh climates).

Rocks and gravel are essential elements of Japanese gardens. These could cost you from $2,000 to $6,000.

Adding fixed structures such as stone lanterns, entrance gates, and fences can add to the authenticity of your Japanese garden. These structures could cost between $5,000 and $20,000.

The cost of transporting and delivering materials to your home can range from $2,000 to $6,000.

The labour costs for landscaping crews can vary greatly, depending on the complexity of the design and the size of your garden. These costs could range from $10,000 to $50,000.

Maintaining a Japanese garden might be challenging for you alone. Thus, you may need to budget between $1,000 and $6,000 for yearly maintenance and upkeep.

There Are Several Other Factors to Consider When Budgeting for Your Japanese Garden:

Depending on the scale of your project and local regulations, you may need to obtain City permits. The cost of these permits can vary.

If your garden design includes water features or outdoor lighting, there will be utility costs to consider, unless you use solar powered features.

As some plants may not survive the winter, you may need to budget for replacing plants or providing them with winter protection. Pests can be a problem in any garden, and you may need to budget for pest control measures.

If you are not a gardening expert, you might need to hire a professional for regular maintenance tasks like pruning, shaping, weeding, etc.

Depending on the value of your garden, especially with expensive structures or rare plants, you might want to consider getting them insured.

The Joy of Self-Taught Japanese Zen Styled Landscaping

By Jose G. Monagas | January 2024 | Calgary, AB Canada

For some of us, traditional college schooling or an apprenticeship can be quite formal and expensive. In contrast, self-learning and lifelong learning can be cost-effective, with so many open and affordable resources available, including books, manuals, online tutorials, forums, blogs, and more. The best learning method depends on your personal learning style, availability, and career goals.

Without the constraints of conventional schooling or an apprenticeship, you have the freedom to explore and experiment. You can learn at your own pace, focusing on those areas that interest you the most. This freedom fosters creativity and innovation, allowing you to develop your unique talents.

This makes Japanese style landscaping accessible to everyone, regardless of race, gender, income, and other circumstances. Without the pressure of grades and attendance, you can tailor your learning schedule according to your lifestyle. Whether you are a working professional, a retiree, or a stay-at-home parent, you can always find time to indulge in your passion for Japanese style landscaping. This also creates an educational environment that celebrates diversity and caters to the needs of every single learner.

More importantly, Japanese Zen style landscaping is more than just gardening -- it is a blend of art and self-awareness. You learn to be patient, observant, and mindful. These requirements not only improve your landscaping skills but also contribute to your personal development. Every expert was once a novice. Thus, learning Japanese style landscaping as a self-taught individual can be a very rewarding experience.

In my case, I highly regard personalized learning and project-based learning. Through them, I can immerse myself on complex, real-world projects that require critical thinking, problem-solving, and collaboration with others, which allows me to explore topics of interest in greater depth. Naturally, there are times when I need a mentor who can provide me with guidance, inspiration, and practical knowledge.

Also, through hands-on learning, I can apply both my personal and professional experience as an invaluable tool in a field that is continually evolving, where practical skills are as important as theoretical knowledge.

As a non-Japanese in Calgary, I know firsthand that understanding the underlying principles of Japanese style landscaping requires time and energy -- and some might find it quite challenging. However, anyone can do well as long as you have a deep respect for the culture, philosophy, and aesthetics while creating spaces that reflect the natural world and bring a sense of peace.

What is more, in navigating the challenges of self-learning traditional Japanese style landscaping in a foreign country, I have found that although there are numerous books and online resources on Japanese style landscaping, not all of them are comprehensive. In fact, some resources may oversimplify or misinterpret the principles of Japanese style landscaping.

Another factor to consider is that there are not many experts in Japanese style landscaping outside of Japan; thus, some learners may struggle to grasp the intricate details of this art form.

Lastly, I cannot skip highlighting the differences in climate and plant availability between Japan and North America. You must be open to learn and adapt Japanese style landscaping principles to local conditions in Calgary. This involves choosing plant species that are similar to those used in Japanese gardens but are suited to the North American climate. You could also modify traditional techniques to work with the local environment where certain plant species may not thrive.

In my experience, self-learning Japanese Zen style landscaping in North America is a feasible and rewarding journey. The key is the ability to learn independently and apply this knowledge creatively. The belief that professional Japanese landscaping requires formal credentials stems from the respect for tradition and mastery. However, this perspective overlooks the potential of self-learning and the value of hands-on experience.
