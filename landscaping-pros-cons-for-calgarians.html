Traditional vs. Japanese Style Landscaping

A Guide for Calgary Homeowners and Condo Corporations

By Jose G. Monagas | Owner, Operator

Ishidoro Zen Gardens Inc

Traditional North American Landscaping

Core Principles

Rooted in European style, traditional North American landscaping emphasizes order, symmetry, and human control over nature. It seeks to create aesthetically pleasing, structured gardens for recreation, socializing, or visual enjoyment. These gardens are typically segmented into defined zones, with neatly arranged paths and boundaries. The layout often reflects a desire to shape nature into a functional and pleasing space for human activities.

Design Basics

Traditional North American gardens are often defined by formal, geometric patterns, such as symmetrical flower beds, trimmed hedges, and neat lawns. The layout is organized into "rooms" or zones, with pathways guiding visitors through the space. Decorative elements like statues, urns, and fountains reflect human creativity and design, serving as focal points within the garden.

Plant Assortment

Traditional North American gardens feature a diverse range of plants, from vibrant flowers to ornamental grasses, creating a colorful, year-round display. Flower beds are often the centerpiece, with plants carefully selected to complement one another in terms of color and texture. Shrubs and trees are also used to enhance the visual appeal, but the emphasis is on variety and visual interest throughout the seasons.

Water Features

Water features in traditional North American gardens are often used for decorative and functional purposes. Fountains, ponds, and swimming pools serve as focal points, enhancing the aesthetic appeal while providing spaces for relaxation or recreation. These elements are frequently embellished with decorative statues or tile work, designed to catch the eye and draw attention.

Hardscape Structures

Traditional North American landscaping uses hard materials like stone, brick, and wood to define the structure of the garden. These materials are used for walkways, fences, patios, and walls, creating clear boundaries between different areas of the garden. The design tends to be more formal, with straight paths and geometric shapes that guide movement through the space.

Care and Upkeep

Traditional North American gardens require regular maintenance, including mowing, trimming, and replanting. Flower beds and lawns need constant attention, and some gardens feature intricate designs, such as topiary or espaliered trees, which demand more frequent care. Fertilizers and pesticides are required to maintain plant health and ensure the garden remains lush and vibrant throughout the seasons.

Cultural Identity

North American gardens often represent human mastery over nature. The focus is on creating spaces that are functional for socializing, relaxation, or recreation, highlighting the gardener's ability to shape and tame the environment. These gardens reflect a desire to control nature and curate an outdoor space that serves human needs.

Color and Aesthetic

Traditional North American gardens are characterized by bold colors and a wide variety of plant species. Flowers are chosen for their vivid hues, creating eye-catching displays throughout the year. The overall aesthetic is cheerful and vibrant, with high-contrast visuals designed to engage the senses. Each plant is selected for its contribution to the overall color scheme and texture of the garden.

Space and Scale

Traditional North American gardens often cover large areas, with expansive lawns, flower beds, and garden rooms laid out for different activities. The design focuses on creating distinct zones for various purposes, such as seating areas, vegetable patches, or water features. Scale is considered, but the emphasis is on providing a multifunctional space for gatherings and outdoor activities.

Sustainability Issues

Traditional North American landscaping can be resource-intensive, particularly when large lawns or extensive water features are involved. Maintaining a green lawn often requires significant water use and chemical treatments. These practices can raise concerns about sustainability and environmental impact, especially in water-scarce areas.

Value vs. Costs

In Calgary, there are over 100 contractors offering their landscaping services, many of whom are trained in horticulture or design. It's important to note that traditional North American landscaping requires a significant investment in labor and materials. Additionally, ongoing expenses like watering, mowing, and pest control can increase utility bills. As a result, maintenance adds to the long-term financial commitment.

Traditional Japanese Style Landscaping

Core Principles

Influenced by Shintoism and Zen Buddhism, Japanese style landscaping is rooted in balance and harmony. It emphasizes working with nature rather than controlling it, creating serene spaces that evoke tranquility and reflection. These gardens are typically asymmetrical, with a focus on the impermanence of nature and creating an environment that feels spiritually enriching and calm.

Design Basics

Japanese gardens embrace asymmetry and natural forms, creating a miniature, abstract landscape that reflects nature's beauty. Rocks, water, and carefully placed plants are key elements, and while the layout may appear random, it evokes a sense of natural harmony. In a traditional Japanese rock garden, for example, rocks represent mountains, and raked gravel symbolizes flowing water.

Plant Assortment

In Japanese gardens, plant selection is restrained, with a focus on symbolism and seasonal beauty. Trees like pines and bamboo are used for their enduring qualities, while seasonal plants such as cherry blossoms and maples highlight the fleeting nature of life. Moss and low-growing plants help create soft ground coverings, enhancing the ambiance and reinforcing the concept of impermanence.

Water Features

In Japanese gardens, water serves as a symbolic element, representing life, renewal, and tranquility. Ponds, streams, and koi fish are integrated into the landscape, designed to blend seamlessly with natural surroundings. The sound of flowing water is an integral part of the garden's vibe, creating a calming, meditative environment that encourages reflection.

Hardscape Structures

Japanese gardens tend to integrate hardscaping elements in a way that feels organic and timeless. They favor natural, rustic materials that blend into the surrounding environment. Stone is used for pathways and rock gardens, while gravel is raked into patterns to symbolize water. Bamboo makes fences or decorative accents, contributing to the garden's overall beauty.

Care and Upkeep

Japanese gardens are lower-maintenance but require mindful care. Features like gravel raking, pruning, and plant care demand attention to detail. In preserving the natural beauty of the garden, pruning is considered an art form. It helps enhance the natural shape of trees and shrubs rather than imposing an artificial form. This helps the garden retain its ambiance without becoming over-maintained.

Cultural Identity

Japanese gardens have deep spiritual and philosophical significance. They are designed to evoke feelings of peace, contemplation, and connection with the natural world. Elements such as rocks symbolize mountains, and water represents the flow of life. They promote mindfulness, offering visitors a means to reflect on their journey.

Color and Aesthetic

Japanese gardens prioritize muted, earthy tones. Greens, grays, and browns dominate the color palette, with occasional splashes of color from seasonal plants like cherry blossoms in spring or vibrant fall foliage. The focus is on creating a calm, reflective atmosphere, where the beauty of the garden is subtle and harmonious, rather than striking or overwhelming.

Space and Scale

Japanese gardens, regardless of size, are designed to create the illusion of greater expansiveness. Techniques such as narrowing paths or layering plants help create a sense of depth and space. The goal is to evoke a feeling of quiet beauty and serenity, even in smaller gardens. Every element is placed with care to ensure a harmonious, tranquil ambiance.

Sustainability Issues

Japanese gardens tend to be environmentally conscious, often utilizing native plants that require less water and maintenance. Water features are designed to be self-sustaining, and materials like moss and gravel reduce the need for excessive irrigation. The focus is on working with nature for sustainability and beauty.

Value vs. Costs

In Calgary, there are fewer contractors offering authentic Japanese style landscaping; many require specific horticulture, cultural, and philosophical training. It's worth mentioning that the upfront investment is comparable to traditional North American landscaping, while long-term maintenance is typically lower. Additionally, minimalist designs help conserve water and reduce overall upkeep. Consequently, maintenance costs are generally lower over time.
